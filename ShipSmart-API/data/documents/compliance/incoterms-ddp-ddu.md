# Incoterms: DDP vs DDU/DAP (who pays duties)

An **Incoterm** defines who is responsible for costs and risk at each stage of an
international shipment. For a small-parcel sender, the choice that matters most is
who pays the destination **duties and import taxes** — the shipper or the
recipient. See `duties-and-taxes.md` for what those charges are.

## DDP — Delivered Duty Paid

The **sender** pays the duties and import taxes up front, so the recipient
receives the parcel with nothing more to pay.

- Best experience for the recipient — no surprise bill on delivery.
- The sender must know (or estimate) the destination duty/tax to price it in.
- Common for cross-border e-commerce that wants a "final price" checkout.

## DDU / DAP — Delivered Duty Unpaid / Delivered At Place

The **recipient** pays the duties and import taxes to customs (usually via the
carrier) before the parcel is released.

- The sender's cost is just shipping; the recipient bears the import charges.
- The recipient may be surprised by a duty/tax + brokerage bill on delivery.
- If the recipient refuses to pay, the parcel can be returned or abandoned.

## Choosing

| You want… | Use |
|---|---|
| Recipient pays nothing extra on delivery | **DDP** |
| Lowest sender cost, recipient handles import charges | **DDU / DAP** |
| A predictable landed-cost checkout | **DDP** |

## What does not change either way

The Incoterm only decides **who pays** — it never changes the obligation to
**declare the goods accurately** (correct value, contents, and HS code). Choosing
DDU does not reduce the duty owed; it only moves who pays it. ShipSmart explains
the trade-off; it does not decide the Incoterm for you or assert a shipment is
compliant.
