# Lithium Batteries & Dangerous Goods

ShipSmart knowledge base — advisory guidance, not legal or carrier certification.

## Why lithium cells are regulated

Lithium batteries are classified as **Class 9 dangerous goods** because they can
short-circuit, overheat, and ignite. Both lithium-ion (rechargeable) and lithium-metal
(primary) cells are regulated for air, ground, and ocean transport. Power banks and
spare/loose batteries are treated as **spare cells** and are subject to the strictest
handling.

## UN numbers

- **UN3480** — lithium-ion batteries shipped on their own.
- **UN3481** — lithium-ion batteries packed with or contained in equipment.
- **UN3090** — lithium-metal batteries shipped on their own.
- **UN3091** — lithium-metal batteries packed with or contained in equipment.

## Watt-hour limits and state of charge

Energy is measured in watt-hours: **Wh = V × Ah**. Consumer devices generally use cells
under the **100 Wh** limit; cells from 100–160 Wh need carrier approval, and above 160 Wh
move as fully regulated Class 9 freight. For air transport, lithium-ion cells shipped on
their own must be at **no more than 30% state of charge**.

## Documentation and marking

Shipments typically require the **Class 9 lithium battery mark**, the correct UN number,
and — depending on quantity and mode — a dangerous-goods declaration prepared under the
applicable IATA (air) or ADR (road) rules. Power banks in carry-on are common for
passengers, but as cargo they are spare cells and may need a declaration.

## Practical checklist

- Identify chemistry (ion vs metal) and Wh rating before quoting.
- Confirm whether the cell ships alone (UN3480/UN3090) or with equipment (UN3481/UN3091).
- Verify the carrier accepts the Wh class on the intended lane and mode.
- Air shipments of standalone cells: confirm ≤30% state of charge.
